--- layout: default title: SleepySnail ---

Automatic detection of sleep in land snails using video tracking and machine learning.

This website presents my data analysis project as part of the MSc in Bioinformatics and Theoretical Systems Biology.

Lay abstract

Sleep appears to be ubiquitous amongst animals. Indeed, most animals in which it has been investigated appear to exhibit and require the basic characteristics of sleep (e.g. quiescent behaviours with elevated arousal thresholds, see C. Cirelli and G. Tononi, 2008). However, literature mainly focusses on a few vertebrate models, and sleep is poorly studied in other phyla (J. M. Siegel, 2008). The development of new experimental paradigms and biological models could provide a significant insight into the functions of sleep. In addition, understanding the evolution of sleep cannot be achieved without studying a wide range of organisms.

Snails are a historically important animal model for studying the cellular basis of learning and memory and they could potentially be used to investigate the links between sleep or sleep deprivation and learning. In mammals, electrophysiology is mostly used to quantify sleep. Since this methodology cannot be applied to other phyla, behavioural observation is generally used. This is however often very time consuming and subjective. The aim of this project was to develop a video processing pipeline to study activity of land snails â€” for which sleep has not yet been characterised. In addition, preliminary analysis of snail day/night activity cycles have been performed.

Scientific abstract

Very few studies have investigated whether molluscs exhibit sleeping pattern, or need sleep to consolidate memory. Behavioural study of sleep requires monitoring the activity of individual animals over several days.

The present study demonstrates an extensible video analysis pipeline which can be used to automatically monitor land snails in future experiments. It explains the important preprocessing steps, such as automatic undistortion, statistical grid detection and time stamp correction. Then, it illustrates the use of Morphological Geodesic Active Contours (P. Marquez-Neila et al., 2013) to detect contours of individual animals in a robust manner (description). This permites quantification of the activity and morphology of 35 individual snails every minute for over 140h.

Binomial Generalised Linear Mixed Models were used to evaluate the effects of variables such as time, age and species on the propensity to be active. This analysis revealed that the overall activity decreases dramatically during the experiment. Snails were expectedly to be significantly more active during the night. Interestingly, young animals were more active than older ones. There were also differences in activity between the two studied snail species.
